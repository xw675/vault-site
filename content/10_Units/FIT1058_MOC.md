## 🧭 Suggested Reading Order

1. [[Set (Mathematics)]] — the basic object: elements, the three ways to specify a set (roster, condition, construction rule), the empty set $\emptyset$, and cardinality $|A|$.
2. [[Sets of Numbers]] ($\mathbb{N}, \mathbb{N}_0, \mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Q}, \mathbb{R}$, the $+/-/_0$ restrictions, and interval notation) and [[Sets of Strings]] (alphabet, string, length, the empty string $\varepsilon$, the power $A^k$ and Kleene star $A^*$) — the two families of sets used throughout computing.
3. [[Subset and Superset]] ($\subseteq, \subset, \supseteq, \supset$; set equality by **double inclusion** $A=B \iff A\subseteq B \wedge B\subseteq A$; the "if and only if" decomposition).
4. [[Power Set]] ($\mathcal{P}(B)$, the $2^n$ subset count, and the **maximum vs maximal** distinction) → [[Binomial Coefficient]] ($\binom{n}{k}$, the closed form $\tfrac{n!}{(n-k)!k!}$, symmetry, and **Pascal's identity**).
5. [[Set Operations (Mathematics)|Set Complement and Difference]] (the universal set $U$, complement $\overline{A}=U\setminus A$, and set difference $B\setminus A$) → [[Set Operations (Mathematics)|Union and Intersection]] ($\cup, \cap$, and **De Morgan's laws**) → [[Set Operations (Mathematics)|Symmetric Difference]] ($A\triangle B$, the "exactly one" / exclusive-or operation).
6. [[Cartesian Product]] (ordered pairs, $A\times B$, $n$-tuples, and $|A\times B|=|A|\,|B|$) and [[Set Partition]] (disjoint nonempty parts covering $A$; the **Bell numbers** counting them).
7. [[Function (Mathematics)]] (domain, codomain, rule; the graph as a subset of $\text{dom}\times\text{codomain}$; identity/indicator/constant functions; multi-argument via a [[Cartesian Product]] domain) → [[Image and Codomain]] (codomain = promise, image = exact outputs; avoid "range").
8. [[Injection, Surjection, Bijection]] (one-to-one / onto / both; lossless vs lossy; finite same-set pigeonhole equivalence) → [[Inverse Function]] (exists iff bijective; swap the pairs; $f^{-1}\circ f=i_A$) → [[Function Composition]] ($g\circ f$, codomain/domain match, non-commutative/associative, socks-and-shoes $(g\circ f)^{-1}=f^{-1}\circ g^{-1}$).
9. [[Cryptosystem]] ($(M,C,K,e,d)$ with $d_k=e_k^{-1}$; cascading ciphers by composition) and [[Counting Functions]] ($n^m$ functions, $\tfrac{n!}{(n-m)!}$ injections, $n!$ bijections).
10. [[Binary Relation]] (a subset of $A\times B$; every function is one; lookup and inverse relations) → [[Properties of Binary Relations]] (reflexive/symmetric/antisymmetric/transitive; composition; transitive closure $R^+$) → [[Equivalence Relation]] (reflexive+symmetric+transitive; equivalence classes; the Fundamental Partition Theorem) → [[n-ary Relation]] (tuples, relational databases, $2^{mn}$ relations).
11. [[Theorem and Proof]] (theorem/lemma/proposition/corollary; a proof as a sequence of definition/assumption/consequence steps; Proof./$\square$) → [[Logical Implication]] ($P\Rightarrow Q\equiv P\subseteq Q$; converse and non-symmetry; bi-implication/iff) → [[Modus Ponens]] (from $P$ and $P\Rightarrow Q$ deduce $Q$).
12. [[Quantifiers (Existential and Universal)]] ($\exists$ proven by one witness, $\forall$ by exhaustion or arbitrary element) → [[Finding Proofs]] (no general algorithm — Gödel/Church/Turing; element-chasing, double-containment, and mutual-inequality blueprints) → [[Proof Techniques]] (symbolic manipulation, construction, cases, contradiction — incl. Euclid's infinitude of primes) → [[Mathematical Induction]] (basis $S(1)$ + step $S(k)\Rightarrow S(k+1)$; $1+\cdots+n=\tfrac{n(n+1)}{2}$; generalized De Morgan; vs statistical induction).
13. [[Proposition and Truth Value]] (statements true/false; truth values $=$ bits; Boolean variables) → [[Logical Connectives]] ($\neg,\wedge,\vee,\Rightarrow,\Leftrightarrow,\oplus$ by truth table; the duals $\wedge\leftrightarrow\cap$, $\vee\leftrightarrow\cup$, $\neg\leftrightarrow$ complement) → [[Exclusive-or]] ($P\oplus Q=\neg(P\Leftrightarrow Q)$; odd parity).
14. [[Boolean Algebra Laws]] (tautology, logical equivalence; the law table with both distributive laws and De Morgan; rewrites $P\Rightarrow Q=\neg P\vee Q$) → [[Disjunctive Normal Form]] (OR-of-ANDs read from the truth table) → [[Conjunctive Normal Form]] (AND-of-ORs; the natural form for rules) → [[Logical Modelling]] (encode rule sets as CNF; "at most/least/exactly $k$ true") → [[Universal Sets of Operations]] ($\{\neg,\wedge,\vee\}$, $\{\neg,\wedge\}$, $\{\neg,\vee\}$ universal; logic gates).
15. [[Predicate]] (a [[Binary Relation]] used as a truth-valued function; arity; equality always available) → [[Term, Variable, and Constant]] (domains; terms = constants/variables/functions of terms; **free vs bound** variables; predicate → proposition when all free variables are assigned) → [[Quantifiers (Existential and Universal)]] (now in predicate logic: domains, binding, the $\exists$-with-$\wedge$ / $\forall$-with-$\Rightarrow$ restriction rule, the $\exists\leftrightarrow\vee$ / $\forall\leftrightarrow\wedge$ analogy).
16. [[Multiple Quantifiers]] (stacking $\exists/\forall$; **mixed order matters** — $\exists\forall\Rightarrow\forall\exists$ but not conversely; distinctness via $=$; instantiation; $\forall/\wedge$ and $\exists/\vee$ distribution) → [[Quantifier Negation]] ($\neg\forall=\exists\neg$, $\neg\exists=\forall\neg$ — De Morgan lifted to first-order logic; "not all dogs are happy" $=$ "there is an unhappy dog").
17. [[Sequence (Mathematics)]] (an ordered list = a [[Function (Mathematics)|function]] on $\mathbb N$; finite/infinite; terms, length; closed form vs recurrence; contrast with an unordered set) → [[Recurrence Relation]] (base case(s) + general case; a $k$-step rule needs $k$ base cases; explore–formulate–prove to a closed form via induction) → [[Arithmetic, Geometric, and Harmonic Sequences]] ($f_n=a+(n-1)d$ linear, $f_n=ar^{n-1}$ exponential; harmonic = reciprocals are arithmetic).
18. [[Fibonacci Sequence]] (two-term recurrence; bounding by induction; the characteristic quadratic $r^2-r-1=0$ and Binet's closed form) → [[Limit of a Sequence]] (the $\forall\varepsilon\,\exists N\,\forall n$ definition; $\lim\tfrac1n=0$; divergent/oscillating cases) → [[Summation Notation]] ($\sum$ anatomy; partial sums; order-free maths vs order-sensitive floating point; bound index) → [[Arithmetic Series]] (general $S_n=na+\tfrac{n(n-1)}2 d$ by reverse-and-add) → [[Geometric Series]] (finite $S_n=a\tfrac{r^n-1}{r-1}$; infinite $\tfrac{a}{1-r}$ iff $|r|<1$) → [[Big-O Notation]] (formal $\exists c,N\,\forall n\ge N$ definition; dominant summand; base matters, constants don't; log rules).
19. [[Divisibility]] ($d\mid n$, multiples $d\mathbb Z$, divisors of sums/combinations; converse fails) → [[Prime Number]] (proper divisors; product-of-primes by strong induction) → [[Modular Arithmetic|Modulo Operation]] ($n=qd+r$, $n\bmod d$, negative-$n$ floor convention; vs `%`) → [[Greatest Common Divisor]] ($\gcd(a,b)=\gcd(b,a-b)$) → [[Euclidean Algorithm]] (subtraction sped to $a\bmod b$).
20. [[Integer Linear Combination]] (Bézout: $\gcd=$ smallest positive $xm+yn$; $m\mathbb Z+n\mathbb Z=\gcd\,\mathbb Z$) → [[Extended Euclidean Algorithm]] (triples $a=xm+yn$ find the coefficients) → [[Coprimality]] ($\gcd=1\iff\exists x,y:xm+yn=1$) → [[Modular Arithmetic]] (congruence $a\equiv b\pmod n$; an equivalence relation; $\mathbb Z_n$ class arithmetic) → [[Modular Inverse]] ($x^{-1}$ exists iff $\gcd(x,n)=1$; via EEA; $\mathbb Z_n^*$).
21. [[Euler Totient Function]] ($\phi(n)=|\mathbb Z_n^*|$; $\phi(p)=p-1$, $\phi(p^k)$, multiplicative; $n\prod(1-1/p_i)$) → [[Modular Exponentiation]] (square-and-multiply $O(\log m)$; reduce mod $n$ and mod $\phi(n)$) → [[Euler's Theorem and Fermat's Little Theorem]] ($x^{\phi(n)}\equiv1$; $x^{p-1}\equiv1$) → [[Primitive Root]] (generator of $\mathbb Z_n^*$; exists for $1,2,4,p^k,2p^k$; $\phi(\phi(n))$ of them) → [[One-Way Function]] (easy forward, hard inverse = Discrete Log; password hashing) → [[Diffie-Hellman Key Agreement]] ($k=a^{x_Ax_B}\bmod p$ over an insecure channel).
22. [[Counting Principles|Addition Principle]] (disjoint alternatives add, $|A\cup B|=|A|+|B|$; sequential loops) → [[Counting Principles|Multiplication Principle]] (independent stages multiply, $|A\times B|=|A||B|$; nested loops) → [[Inclusion-Exclusion Principle]] (overlapping sets: alternating signed sum $\sum_k(-1)^{k+1}$ over $k$-fold intersections; induction proof) → [[Selection (Counting Framework)]] (order × replacement 2×2: $n^r$, $(n)_r$, $\binom{n}{r}$, $\binom{r+n-1}{r}$) → [[Stars and Bars]] (unordered with replacement; $x_i\ge0$ solutions of $\sum x_i=r$; the $+1$ bijection giving $\binom{r+n-1}{n-1}$).
23. [[Sample Space and Events]] (experiment, outcomes, $U$ as universal set; events are subsets; choosing a uniform fine-grained space) → [[Probability]] ($\mathrm{Pr}(x)\ge0$, $\sum=1$, $\mathrm{Pr}(A)=\sum_{x\in A}\mathrm{Pr}(x)$; non-uniform and infinite distributions via geometric series) → [[Probability|Equally Likely Outcomes]] ($\mathrm{Pr}(A)=|A|/|U|$ — probability as counting) → [[Mutually Exclusive Events]] (disjoint $\Rightarrow$ additive $\mathrm{Pr}(A\sqcup B)=\mathrm{Pr}A+\mathrm{Pr}B$) → [[Probability Rules for Events]] (complement $1-\mathrm{Pr}(\overline A)$; difference; general union $\mathrm{Pr}A+\mathrm{Pr}B-\mathrm{Pr}(A\cap B)$).
24. [[Independent Events]] ($\mathrm{Pr}(A\cap B)=\mathrm{Pr}A\,\mathrm{Pr}B$; independent $\ne$ mutually exclusive; series $p^2$, parallel $2p-p^2$) → [[Conditional Probability]] ($\mathrm{Pr}(A\mid B)=\mathrm{Pr}(A\cap B)/\mathrm{Pr}(B)$; restricted-and-rescaled sample space) → [[Law of Total Probability]] ($\mathrm{Pr}(B)=\sum_i\mathrm{Pr}(B\mid A_i)\mathrm{Pr}(A_i)$ over a partition) → [[Bayes' Theorem]] ($\mathrm{Pr}(A\mid B)=\mathrm{Pr}(B\mid A)\mathrm{Pr}(A)/\mathrm{Pr}(B)$; extended form; prior → posterior belief updating).
25. [[Random Variable]] (a function $U\to\mathbb R$; "$X=k$" is an event; distribution $\mathrm{Pr}(X=k)$; sums and independent RVs) → [[Expectation]] ($E(X)=\sum_k k\,\mathrm{Pr}(X=k)$; $E(c)=c$, $E(\alpha X)=\alpha E(X)$; **linearity** $E(X+Y)=E(X)+E(Y)$; $E(XY)=E(X)E(Y)$ if independent) → [[Median and Mode]] (the "middle" $\mathrm{Pr}(X\le m),\mathrm{Pr}(X\ge m)\ge\tfrac12$, and the most-likely value; robust vs outlier-sensitive) → [[Variance and Standard Deviation]] ($\mathrm{Var}=E((X-\mu)^2)=E(X^2)-\mu^2$; $\sigma=\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}}$; additive if independent; Chebyshev $\mathrm{Pr}(|X-\mu|\ge t\sigma)\le1/t^2$).
26. [[Uniform Distribution]] ($\mathrm{Unif}(a,b)$; $E=\tfrac{a+b}2$, $\mathrm{Var}=\tfrac{(b-a+1)^2-1}{12}$; fairness or ignorance) → [[Binomial Distribution|Bernoulli Trial]] ($\{0,1\}$, $E=p$, $\mathrm{Var}=p(1-p)$) → [[Binomial Distribution]] ($\mathrm{Bin}(n,p)$; $\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$; $E=np$, $\mathrm{Var}=np(1-p)$ via summands) → [[Poisson Distribution]] ($e^{-\mu}\mu^k/k!$; $E=\mathrm{Var}=\mu$; large-$n$ small-$np$ binomial approx) → [[Geometric Distribution]] ($(1-p)^{k-1}p$; $E=1/p$, $\mathrm{Var}=\tfrac{1-p}{p^2}$; memoryless) → [[Coupon Collector's Problem]] ($E(Z)=nH_n$ by linearity over geometric stages).
27. [[Graph]] (vertices + edges $\{v,w\}$; adjacency $\sim$, incidence; isolated/leaf) → [[Types of Graphs]] (simple = no loops/parallel edges; weighted; directed) → [[Graph Representations]] (edge list, adjacency matrix/list, incidence matrix; dense vs sparse) → [[Subgraph]] ($U\subseteq V$, $D\subseteq E$; $F\le G$, proper $F<G$) → [[Special Graphs]] ($K_n$, $\overline{K_n}$, $P_{n-1}$, $C_n$) → [[Degree and the Handshaking Lemma]] ($\sum\deg=2m$; average $2m/n$; two equal degrees; even # odd-degree).
28. [[Walks, Trails, and Paths]] (walk $\supseteq$ trail (no repeat edge) $\supseteq$ path (no repeat vertex); distance = shortest path) → [[Cycle (Graph Theory)]] (closed trail, no interior repeat; odd closed walk $\iff$ odd cycle) → [[Connectivity]] (path-connected; components = maximal connected subgraphs = classes of the walk equivalence relation) → [[Bipartite Graph]] ($V=A\sqcup B$ $\iff$ 2-colourable $\iff$ no odd cycle) → [[Euler Tour]] (closed trail using every edge; exists iff connected & all even degree; Königsberg).
29. [[Tree]] (connected acyclic graph; minimal connected; $n-1$ edges; has a leaf; unique paths) → [[Forest]] (acyclic graph; disjoint trees; $n-k$ edges) → [[Spanning Tree]] (tree subgraph reaching all vertices; delete/add-edge methods) → [[Kruskal's Greedy Algorithm]] (cheapest cycle-free edge each step; minimum-cost spanning tree; greedy yet optimal) → [[Planar Graph]] (drawable with no crossings; faces, boundaries, sides) → [[Euler's Formula (Planar Graphs)]] ($n-m+f=2$; $m\le3n-6$, triangle-free $m\le2n-4$; $K_5$ and $K_{3,3}$ nonplanar).

## 🎯 Learning Outcomes

After Week 1 you should be able to: specify sets three ways and compute cardinalities; use the number/string set families and interval notation; reason with $\subseteq/\subset$ and prove set equality by double inclusion; count subsets with the power set ($2^n$) and binomial coefficients (incl. Pascal's identity); and manipulate the set operations (complement, difference, union, intersection, symmetric difference, Cartesian product, partition) including De Morgan's laws.

After Week 2 you should be able to: define a function by domain/codomain/rule and distinguish codomain from image; classify functions as injective/surjective/bijective and decide when an inverse exists; compose functions (and invert composites by socks-and-shoes); model a cryptosystem as keyed invertible functions; count functions, injections, and bijections; and work with binary/$n$-ary relations — their properties (reflexive/symmetric/antisymmetric/transitive), composition and closure, equivalence relations and the partition they induce, and the $2^{mn}$ relation count.

After Week 3 you should be able to: explain what theorems and proofs are and check a proof's validity (verifiability, sequential readability); reason with implication ($P\Rightarrow Q\equiv P\subseteq Q$, the converse trap, iff) and apply modus ponens; prove and disprove existential and universal statements; choose a proof method (symbolic manipulation, construction, cases, contradiction) for a given goal; and carry out proofs by mathematical induction, distinguishing it from empirical/statistical induction.

After Week 4 you should be able to: identify propositions and Boolean variables; give and use the truth tables of the connectives ($\neg,\wedge,\vee,\Rightarrow,\Leftrightarrow,\oplus$) and their set duals; apply the laws of Boolean algebra (incl. both distributive laws and De Morgan) to simplify and to test equivalence; convert expressions to DNF and CNF and explain why every expression has each; model rule sets (including "at most/least/exactly $k$ true") as CNF; and identify universal operation sets ($\{\neg,\wedge,\vee\}$, $\{\neg,\wedge\}$, $\{\neg,\vee\}$) and their relevance to logic gates.

After Week 5 you should be able to: use predicates (relations as truth-valued functions) and build well-formed terms; distinguish free from bound variables and know when a predicate becomes a proposition; quantify over a domain with the correct restriction connective ($\exists$ with $\wedge$, $\forall$ with $\Rightarrow$); reason with multiple quantifiers (respecting that mixed order matters, and the instantiation/distribution rules); and negate quantified statements by flipping each quantifier ($\neg\forall=\exists\neg$, $\neg\exists=\forall\neg$).

After Week 6 you should be able to: treat a sequence as a function on $\mathbb N$ and define one by closed form or recurrence; set up recurrences with the right number of base cases and convert them to closed forms by explore–formulate–prove with induction; handle arithmetic, geometric, harmonic and Fibonacci sequences (incl. Binet's formula); apply the $\varepsilon$–$N$ definition of a limit; use summation notation and derive the arithmetic ($na+\tfrac{n(n-1)}2 d$) and geometric ($a\tfrac{r^n-1}{r-1}$, and $\tfrac{a}{1-r}$ for $|r|<1$) series formulas; and apply Big-O from its formal $\exists c,N$ definition, keeping the dominant term while dropping constant factors (but not exponential bases).

After Week 7 you should be able to: reason with divisibility, primes (existence of prime factorisation), and the modulo operation; compute gcds with the Euclidean Algorithm and Bézout coefficients with the Extended Euclidean Algorithm; characterise and test coprimality ($xm+yn=1$); work in $\mathbb Z_n$ — congruence as an equivalence relation, class arithmetic, and modular inverses (exist iff coprime); compute the Euler totient $\phi(n)$ from a factorisation and apply Euler's theorem / Fermat's Little Theorem to reduce exponents; perform fast modular exponentiation; identify primitive roots and one-way functions (modular exp / Discrete Log); and explain Diffie-Hellman key agreement and the problem an eavesdropper faces.

After Week 8 you should be able to: count by addition (disjoint alternatives) and multiplication (independent stages), and recognise which applies; apply the inclusion–exclusion principle to overlapping sets (two, three, and the general $\sum_k(-1)^{k+1}$ form, with its inductive proof); classify a selection by order and replacement and use the right formula — $n^r$, the falling factorial $(n)_r$, $\binom{n}{r}$, and the stars-and-bars count $\binom{r+n-1}{r}$; and count nonnegative integer solutions of $x_1+\cdots+x_n=r$ via the stars-and-bars bijection.

After Week 9 you should be able to: set up a sample space and events, and assign probabilities (uniform $|A|/|U|$, non-uniform, and infinite distributions normalised by a geometric series); add probabilities over mutually exclusive events and apply the complement/difference/union rules; test independence via $\mathrm{Pr}(A\cap B)=\mathrm{Pr}(A)\mathrm{Pr}(B)$ and distinguish it from mutual exclusivity (e.g. series/parallel reliability); compute conditional probabilities $\mathrm{Pr}(A\mid B)$; use the law of total probability over a partition; and apply Bayes' Theorem (and its extended form) to update prior beliefs into posteriors.

After Week 10 you should be able to: define a random variable and its probability distribution, and combine random variables (sums, independence); compute the expectation and use its linearity (and the product rule for independent variables); find the median and mode and contrast them with the mean as measures of location; compute variance and standard deviation (both formulas), add variances of independent variables, and apply Chebyshev's inequality; and recognise and use the standard distributions — uniform, Bernoulli, binomial ($E=np$, $\mathrm{Var}=np(1-p)$), Poisson ($E=\mathrm{Var}=\mu$, binomial approximation), and geometric (memoryless, $E=1/p$) — including the coupon-collector expectation $nH_n$.

After Week 11 you should be able to: model a system as a graph and use adjacency/incidence, isolated/leaf vertices; distinguish simple graphs from loop/multi-edge/weighted/directed variants and represent a graph four ways (edge list, adjacency matrix/list, incidence matrix); work with subgraphs and the standard families ($K_n$, $\overline{K_n}$, $P_{n-1}$, $C_n$); compute degrees and apply the Handshaking Lemma ($\sum\deg=2m$, average $2m/n$, the equal-degree and even-odd-degree corollaries); reason about walks, trails, paths, distance, cycles, and connectivity (components as equivalence classes); test bipartiteness via 2-colouring / no odd cycle; and apply Euler's theorem (closed edge-covering trail iff connected with all-even degrees) as in the Königsberg bridges.

After Week 12 you should be able to: define a tree (connected acyclic graph) and use its key properties (minimal connected, $n-1$ edges, has a leaf, unique paths) and the rooted/unrooted distinction; recognise forests and count their edges ($n-k$); construct spanning trees by adding or deleting edges, and find a minimum-cost spanning tree with Kruskal's greedy algorithm (and appreciate why greedy is optimal here); and analyse planar graphs via faces and Euler's formula $n-m+f=2$, applying the bounds $m\le3n-6$ and (triangle-free) $m\le2n-4$ to prove $K_5$ and $K_{3,3}$ nonplanar.
